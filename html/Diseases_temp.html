Description: This is a highly contagious and often fatal viral disease caused by the feline parvovirus. It primarily affects kittens and unvaccinated cats, attacking rapidly dividing cells in the body, such as those in the intestines and bone marrow.

Symptoms: Severe diarrhea, vomiting, dehydration, fever, lethargy, and loss of appetite. Due to the destruction of white blood cells, affected cats are vulnerable to secondary infections.

Management and Preventive Care: Vaccination is the most effective way to prevent feline panleukopenia. There is no specific cure, so treatment involves supportive care, such as intravenous fluids, nutritional support, and antibiotics for secondary infections, to help the cat's immune system fight the virus.

When to see the Vet: This is a medical emergency. If you suspect your cat has panleukopenia, seek veterinary care immediately. Early and aggressive treatment is crucial for survival.

Description: FLUTD refers to a group of conditions affecting a cat's bladder and urethra. It can be caused by urinary stones, infections, or inflammation. Stress is often a contributing factor.

Symptoms: Straining to urinate, frequent urination in small amounts, urinating outside the litter box, bloody urine, and crying out while urinating. A urinary blockage is a life-threatening emergency.

Management and Preventive Care: Treatment depends on the cause and may include dietary changes, increasing water intake, stress reduction, and medications. Environmental enrichment, such as providing multiple litter boxes and clean water sources, is key.

When to see the Vet: Contact a vet immediately if you notice any signs, especially if your cat is straining to urinate but producing little to no urine. This could indicate a dangerous blockage.

Description: Often compared to HIV in humans, FIV is a virus that weakens a cat's immune system, making it susceptible to secondary infections. It is primarily transmitted through deep bite wounds from infected cats.

Symptoms: Early symptoms can be mild and go unnoticed. As the disease progresses, signs may include fever, weight loss, enlarged lymph nodes, poor coat condition, and chronic infections of the skin, gums, or respiratory tract.

Management and Preventive Care: There is no cure for FIV. Management focuses on keeping the cat healthy, providing a stress-free indoor environment, and promptly treating secondary infections. Regular vet check-ups are essential. The best prevention is to keep cats indoors to avoid fights.

When to see the Vet: Any cat that has been in a fight or shows signs of chronic illness should be tested for FIV. While not an immediate emergency, a diagnosis requires long-term management with a vet.

Symptoms: Common signs include weight loss despite an increased appetite, increased thirst and urination, hyperactivity, vomiting, and a poor hair coat. If left untreated, it can lead to heart disease and high blood pressure.
